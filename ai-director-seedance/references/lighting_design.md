## Lighting priority

Treat lighting as narrative structure, not as decoration.

When planning a shot, decide in this order:

1. what the audience should feel
2. what the audience must see clearly
3. where the light is motivated from
4. how much contrast the scene can tolerate
5. what the material response should feel like

Do not start by naming random fixtures or generic “cinematic lighting.”

## Emotional lighting strategies

Map emotion to visible light behavior.

### Comfort or healing

- soft key
- low-contrast fill
- warm or warm-neutral color bias
- gentle separation light
- stable exposure with no harsh clipping

Good for:

- cozy brand films
- friendship and gift moments
- spring and family warmth

### Vulnerability or tenderness

- soft but directional key
- modest negative fill on one side
- slightly dimmer background
- controlled specular highlights

Good for:

- confessions
- apology beats
- emotional close-ups

### Wonder or magic

- motivated practical base plus one stylized accent
- visible rim or edge light
- selective glow in particles, haze, or atmosphere
- controlled highlight bloom

Good for:

- fantasy reveals
- magical transformations
- moonlight or sacred-object moments

### Tension or threat

- stronger key-to-fill contrast
- narrower beam or more directional source
- colder or mixed color temperature
- reduced background lift
- less escape space in the shadows

Good for:

- suspicion
- danger
- emotional pressure

### Joy or release

- brighter exposure
- broader fill
- clearer eye light
- cleaner separation from background
- more readable color contrast

Good for:

- payoff moments
- celebratory reveals
- successful product or brand endings

## Key, fill, rim, and background relationships

Describe the roles, not just the labels.

### Key light

The dominant shaping light. It tells the audience where emotional emphasis lives.

Use key light to define:

- face readability
- volume
- drama level
- direction of attention

### Fill light

The control for contrast and emotional softness.

Use fill light to decide:

- how forgiving the face feels
- how open the shadows are
- whether the scene feels clean, tense, or intimate

### Rim or edge light

The separation light. It should create contour, not halo spam.

Use rim light when:

- the character needs clean separation from a busy background
- magical or premium polish is needed
- fur, hair, cotton, haze, or particles should glow at the edge

### Background light

The depth organizer. It controls hierarchy behind the subject.

Use background light to:

- keep the world readable
- create gradient depth
- isolate the hero subject
- avoid muddy, flat backgrounds

## Lighting ratios

Use lighting ratios as an emotional simplifier.

Practical working ranges:

- 1:1 to 2:1 -> open, soft, friendly, cosmetic, lifestyle
- 2:1 to 4:1 -> naturalistic, cinematic, balanced drama
- 4:1 to 8:1 -> strong drama, mystery, pressure
- 8:1 and above -> stylized tension or harsh isolation

For prompt work, do not usually write numeric ratios directly.

Translate them into visible outcomes:

- very soft low-contrast light
- moderate contrast with readable shadow detail
- deep shadow side with minimal fill

## Color temperature

Treat color temperature as relationship, not a single number.

Use warm versus cool contrast to create emotional depth.

Common patterns:

- warm key + cooler background -> intimate subject, airy world
- cool ambient + warm practical accents -> night scenes with emotional focus
- neutral daylight + warm bounce -> honest naturalism with softness
- warm sunset + soft neutral fill -> premium lifestyle warmth

Avoid writing exact Kelvin values unless technical precision matters. Prefer visible phrasing:

- warm golden key
- cool blue ambient shadow
- neutral daylight base with warm practical accents

## Motivated sources

Every important light should feel motivated by the world, even in fantasy.

Typical motivators:

- sun
- overcast sky
- window
- lantern
- candle
- moonlight
- practical bulbs
- magical object
- reflective ground or walls

Fantasy rule:

Start from a believable base source, then add one stylized accent.

Good:

- warm sunset as base, osmanthus glow as accent
- moonlight base, jade bottle glow as accent

Bad:

- random glow from every direction with no narrative cause

## Material behavior

Plan light based on the surface response.

### Cotton, fur, fabric

- likes soft key and edge light
- benefits from backlight or rim for fluff and fiber separation
- too much hard frontal light makes it flat and dusty

### Skin or soft creature faces

- benefits from soft directional light and controlled fill
- needs gentle eye lights for emotional readability

### Glass, bottle, polished enamel

- needs controlled reflections and clean highlight shape
- benefits from large soft sources or strip-like reflections
- avoid random clutter reflections

### Metal or glossy trim

- read through specular shape more than local color
- use cleaner, more intentional highlights
- keep highlight count low for premium feel

### Haze, dust, particles, cotton fluff

- needs backlight, sidelight, or volumetric shafts
- front light alone kills depth

## Animation, fantasy, and advertising layering

### Animation

Prioritize silhouette clarity and readable planes before realism.

Use:

- cleaner key direction
- more intentional color separation
- readable eye lights
- simplified shadow design

### Fantasy

Build from motivated realism, then stylize one layer.

Use:

- atmospheric rim
- selective glow
- controlled bloom
- symbolic color accents

### Advertising

Use lighting to elevate cleanliness and desirability.

Use:

- intentional highlight placement
- soft broad key for premium surfaces
- readable brand colors
- clear subject-background separation
- reduced visual mud in the darks

## Premium commercial lighting

For “高级感而不脏”, aim for controlled contrast and clean highlight logic.

Do:

- keep the darks rich but not muddy
- separate subject from background with subtle edge or background gradients
- use fewer, cleaner highlight families
- make color contrast deliberate, not noisy
- let premium surfaces breathe with soft, shaped reflections

Avoid:

- too many competing glows
- muddy warm-cool mixtures everywhere
- crushed shadows with no material detail
- random neon accents unrelated to the brand
- over-bloom that destroys texture

## Seedance translation

Translate lighting strategy into visible results, not crew notes.

Good:

- warm sunset backlight through cotton fibers
- soft directional key with gentle shadow side
- moonlit rim on fluffy edges
- low-contrast premium product lighting with clean reflections

Avoid:

- key light 45 degrees camera left with 4:1 ratio and 5600K

That may be useful in a lighting plan, but is weaker as a generation prompt unless the system was explicitly trained on such notation.

If needed, keep technical notes in the structured plan and convert them to visible outcomes in the final prompt.
