## Establish the scene objective

Name the scene objective before choosing any camera grammar.

Examples:

- establish isolation
- reveal threat
- stage a confession
- clarify spatial geography
- sell product desire
- heighten comic timing

If the objective is unclear, delay prompt writing and resolve the objective first.

## Decide blocking before camera

Before deciding the shot size or movement, write down:

- where the protagonist starts
- where support characters start
- who moves first
- whether the story wants distance, approach, support, pursuit, or separation
- when the visual center should transfer

Do not let the camera compensate for unresolved staging.

If the beat works better because the actor moves and the camera waits, prefer that over ornamental movement.

## Choose a shot count range

Do not design too many shots by default.

Use a rough heuristic:

- short emotional beat: 1-3 shots
- standard dialogue scene: 3-8 shots
- action beat: 4-10 shots
- ad or promo beat: 2-6 shots

Prefer fewer stronger shots over many redundant shots.

## Order the shots by dramatic function

Use a function-first sequence:

1. establish geography or emotional premise
2. direct attention to the key action or relationship
3. reveal the turning point, reaction, or consequence
4. add insert or detail only if it changes meaning

Do not force every scene to use all four steps.

## Record composition goals and focus shifts

For each shot, explicitly state:

- who owns the frame at the start
- what the frame should make the audience notice first
- whether the shot transfers attention from one subject to another
- how that transfer happens: blocking, camera path, reframing, or lighting emphasis

If the focus shift is ambiguous, the shot is not ready.

## Validate action timing before locking the shot

Do not assume a shot is readable just because the duration looks short.

Check whether the available seconds can clearly hold:

- setup
- execution
- result
- reaction

Questions:

- Does the audience first understand what the action is aimed at?
- If a second character acts, do they get a readable preparation beat?
- Does the result have enough hold time to be understood?
- Is the reaction separated enough from the result to read emotionally?
- Is the shot trying to carry two major action chains at once?

If a beat includes an attempt and a visible failure, treat the result hold as mandatory.
If one character hands off to another, treat the handoff as its own readable mini-beat.

When the action chain is overloaded, prefer:

- fewer actions in the shot
- a longer hold on result
- a clearer handoff
- a simpler camera move

## Escalate or relax camera behavior deliberately

Tie movement and framing changes to the scene arc.

Typical patterns:

- uncertainty -> wider or more withheld framing
- intimacy -> closer shot size and cleaner backgrounds
- pressure -> tighter framing, stronger contrast, less escape space
- spectacle -> wider frames, layered depth, controlled movement
- release -> more air, softer movement, more readable composition

Tie the movement choice to a reason:

- push in because the character commits
- dolly out because emotional distance grows
- lateral move because relationship geometry changes
- follow because the subject action leads the beat
- locked frame because stillness creates force

If the shot cannot justify the movement in one sentence, simplify it.

## Critique, correct, and verify

Run a revision pass before finalizing:

1. critique the first shot idea
2. identify the likely failure mode
3. correct the shot with the smallest change that fixes the issue
4. verify that protagonist priority, dramatic clarity, and controllability improved

Common failures:

- movement is decorative rather than motivated
- the wrong subject owns frame center
- shot size says less than the script needs
- composition is crowded when the moment wants clarity
- rhythm rushes past a reaction beat
- action chain skips setup, result, or reaction
- the prompt asks the generator to do too many things at once

## Translate to Seedance-ready shot text

For each shot, make sure the final prompt preserves:

- subject identity
- action priority
- location and time cues
- visible style traits
- shot grammar
- continuity anchors

Do not add camera jargon unless it visibly changes the output.
Do not finalize a shot until you can explain why its blocking, composition, movement, and tempo are the simplest strong version of the beat.
