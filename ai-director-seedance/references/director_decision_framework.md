# Director Decision Framework

## Purpose

Use this file when the user wants more than complete prompts.

The goal is to force the skill to think like a disciplined director or storyboard artist:

- identify what the beat means
- choose staging before camera decoration
- justify camera choices from dramatic need
- keep the protagonist hierarchy correct

This file complements the shot taxonomy. It is not a prompt template.

## Core rule

Do not start from a movement label.

Start from the dramatic beat, then blocking, then composition, then camera.

The camera should answer the staging problem, not replace it.

## Decision stack

For each shot, record these decisions in order:

1. `narrative_purpose`
   What this shot must accomplish in story terms.

2. `audience_focus`
   What the audience must notice first.

3. `blocking_plan`
   Where each key subject starts, who moves first, who remains primary, and whether the camera follows or waits.

4. `composition_goal`
   What the frame hierarchy should be. State who owns the center or dominant weight.

5. `focus_shift`
   If attention transfers, state from whom to whom, and why.

6. `movement_motivation`
   Why the camera moves, or why it does not move.

7. `action_timing_validation`
   Whether the available duration can clearly carry setup, execution, result, and reaction.

8. `tempo`
   How the shot should feel in time: withheld, measured, gentle, suspended, decisive, accelerating, abrupt, floating.

9. `transition_reason`
   Why this shot should cut or hand off to the next shot.

## Professional questions

Before finalizing a shot, answer these:

- Why this shot instead of a simpler static shot?
- Why this size instead of one step wider or tighter?
- Why should the audience look here first?
- Does blocking express the relationship before the camera does?
- Is the helper or supporting character stealing the protagonist's frame weight?
- Is the visual center transfer intentional and legible?
- Is the camera moving because the subject or story shifts, not because movement feels cinematic?
- Does the shot hold long enough for the emotional beat to land?
- Does the shot give enough readable time for preparation, execution, result, and reaction?
- If a second character acts, does that handoff get its own readable setup?

## Movement logic

Use movement only when it serves one of these:

- commitment
- hesitation
- pursuit
- reveal
- concealment
- spatial clarification
- emotional approach
- emotional withdrawal

Avoid decorative movement when:

- the beat is about stillness
- the beat is already clear through blocking
- the generator will likely lose identity consistency during unnecessary motion

## Protagonist hierarchy

If the script assigns one subject as the true lead of the beat:

- keep that subject closer to frame center or stronger in contrast
- let supporting characters enter from edge, secondary plane, or side weight
- move the camera with the protagonist decision, not the support entrance
- only transfer visual priority if the story emphasis truly changes

## Commercial and brand films

In brand storytelling, a strong shot must do three things at once:

- preserve emotional clarity
- preserve product or brand tone
- preserve generator controllability

If one shot tries to carry too much exposition, emotion, magic, and branding, split it.
